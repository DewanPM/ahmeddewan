Research

Master thesis summary

My master's thesis focusing on Staphylococcus aureus (S. aureus), a prominent human pathogen with significant clinical implications. It outlines the bacterium's characteristics, including its grape-like clusters and its capacity to cause a range of infections, particularly concerning its increasing antibiotic resistance, notably in the form of methicillin-resistant S. aureus (MRSA). This resistance presents a major public health challenge, contributing to high morbidity and mortality rates globally, which necessitates urgent action from health authorities.

The thesis elaborates on the adaptive mechanisms of S. aureus, particularly its ability to form biofilms that shield it from both the immune system and the effects of antibiotic treatments. Different strains of S. aureus exhibit varying resistance profiles influenced by their genetic makeup, with the mecA gene being a key factor for methicillin resistance, enabling survival despite antibiotic exposure.

In addressing the threat posed by S. aureus, the thesis proposes alternative therapeutic strategies, including phage therapy, which utilizes bacteriophages to specifically target and eliminate pathogenic bacteria. Additionally, it explores the antimicrobial potential of plant extracts from traditional medicinal plants such as Nigella sativa (black cumin) and Lawsonia inermis (henna),assessing their efficacy in inhibiting S. aureus growth.

The research aims to achieve multiple objectives, including the isolation and identification of S. aureus from clinical samples, the determination of virulence genes, antibiotic sensitivity testing, and evaluating the effects of plant extracts and bacteriophages on S. aureus isolates. The thesis underscores the critical need for innovative approaches to combat antimicrobial resistance and highlights the importance of understanding the molecular characteristics of S. aureus in the development of effective treatment strategies.
